## The Display project 

<!-- add a presentation slide -->

/** Notes **/

The Display project proposes a semantic infrastructure for the structured documentation of exhibition hangings. It was developed within the Ouvroir for Digital Art History and Museology under the scientific direction of Emmanuel Château-Dutier. I served as the project lead, and David Valentine was the ontologist. This work was conducted as part of the CIÉCO Partnership, *New Uses of Collections in Art Museums*, which brings together some twenty researchers and six Canadian museums. Marie Fraser, in particular, leads a research axis on the history of collection hangings, for which this tool was developed.

===>>>>>>===

## Problem Statement

How can we reconstruct an exhibition hanging when its documentation is often fragmented, heterogeneous, and sometimes even contradictory? How can we rigorously and systematically leverage these sources?

/** Notes **/

The starting question is simple, but the practical challenges are considerable: How can we reconstruct an exhibition hanging when its documentation is often fragmented, heterogeneous, and sometimes even contradictory? How can we rigorously and systematically leverage these sources?

We hypothesize that a web interface tailored to researchers' workflows can enable non-experts in semantic web technologies to produce structured data according to a formal ontological model, with quality comparable to that achieved by expert methods. Our contribution is twofold: methodological, by documenting a design process to make the semantic web accessible to humanities researchers, and empirical, by demonstrating it through the *Feux pâles* exhibition.

I will first present the problem statement, then the project's methodology, architecture, and results, and finally, the perspectives.

===vvvvvv===

## Problem Statement

![alt text](../img/problematique.png)

/** Notes **/

- Existing documentation: rich but unstructured
- Lack of a model for the spatiality of hangings
- Inaccessibility of the semantic web for humanities researchers

These past years, institutions like MoMA or Tate have undertaken ambitious projects to document the history of their exhibitions. The MoMA Exhibition History Project documents over 3,500 exhibitions since 1929 with photographs, press releases, and lists of works. With the Google Arts & Culture Lab, they used an algorithm to analyze more than 30,000 exhibition photos, searching for matches with the 65,000 works in their online collection. But these projects focus on providing primary documentation: the spatial relationships between exhibited works are not formally described. Projects like UCLA's Virtual Museum reconstruct exhibitions in 3D but without structuring the data, which limits any systematic analysis of hanging practices.

On the documentary modeling side, CIDOC-CRM offers a generic event-oriented ontology. More recent work, such as Onto-Exhibit, focuses on specific dimensions of exhibitions. However, there was no specialized model for the spatial documentation of hangings.

This is not a minor issue. Collection management systems can list works and exhibitions but rarely model where the works were or how they related to each other in space. The hanging plans sometimes found in archives are production tools—they serve to size walls, not to document for research. Display does not aim to replace this documentation or create mimetic 3D models but to address the needs of historical research.

The third challenge is accessibility. The semantic web offers considerable potential for structuring cultural data, but its technical complexity limits its adoption. Display proposes to hide (without erasing) this complexity behind interfaces designed for humanities researchers.

In short, what we aim to do is help art historians study exhibition hangings to facilitate their analysis while allowing them to reuse their data.

## Methodology

![alt text](../img/process.png)

/** Notes **/

The development of Display did not begin with abstract technical specifications. It started in the field, based on concrete research situations. The approach was built in three phases, which seem rather classic: from the field to ontology, and from ontology to interface.

The first phase involved identifying real needs. We worked with Marie Fraser's team to understand their documentation practices. My own 2017 research on the *Feux pâles* exhibition served as a pilot case. It took place in 1990 at the CAPC Musée d’art contemporain de Bordeaux, curated by PT under the title *readymade appartiennent à tout le monde*. It was a major group exhibition: 96 works, 82 artists, complex scenography, and rich but fragmented archival documentation.

A second phase focused on formal modeling. Regular workshops between art historians, semantic web specialists, and developers allowed us to progressively refine the ontological model. As you know, this collaborative approach is essential: the ontology must meet research needs while maintaining formal rigor compatible with international standards.

Finally, a third phase involved developing the interface, carried out with the company Tractr. Several cycles of user testing allowed us to adjust the features and ergonomics for this first prototype version. This iterative process revealed the importance of certain choices: 2D and 3D visualization, explicit management of alternative hypotheses, and direct annotation of sources.

## Display

![alt text](../img/display-app-instantiation.png)

/** Notes **/

The application's architecture is based on creating a SPARQL endpoint using the Apache Jena framework, combined with an inference engine. The use of CRAFTS enabled the creation of a REST API for data access via JSON-LD, following models close to those proposed by Linked Art. The web client offers three distinct views for data entry and visualization: a 3D scene developed with Three.js, a module for processing documentary sources, and a view of structured data. The interface design is inspired by the methods used by researchers to document exhibitions and allows exploring different hypotheses based on the data model.

## The Display Ontology

A conceptualization of the **topology of the exhibition**:

- The concept of *Exhibit*: an object situated in an exhibition space
- **Topological relations** between objects and spaces
- `OWL` ontology, specialization of the architectural ontology `BOT`, and compatible with `CIDOC-CRM`

Accessible at: [https://ouvroir.github.io/display-ontology/](https://ouvroir.github.io/display-ontology/)

/** Notes **/

The core of the project is based on a domain ontology that models the topology of the exhibition.

Its central conceptual unit is the *Exhibit* (or "expôt" in French), an object that can be situated in an exhibition space, whether it is a work of art or a scenographic element. From there, the ontology defines a vocabulary of abstract topological relations that describe how objects are positioned relative to each other and how they are situated in space: adjacency, contiguity, facing, nesting.

Technically, this ontology is expressed in OWL, which allows applying descriptive logic to the model. It relies on a specialization of the Building Topology Ontology (BOT) for space description and maintains compatibility with CIDOC-CRM for metadata about works.

## Ontological Modeling as a Historian’s Practice

- **The Open World Assumption**: Embracing incomplete and contradictory sources as structural data.
- **Multiple Instantiation**: Reconciling the **spatial** view (BOT) and the **event-based** view (CIDOC-CRM).
- **Inference as a Heuristic Tool**: Deducing implicit relations (transitivity, property chains) to enrich analysis.
- **Modeling is Interpreting**: Formalization forces the explicit statement of historiographical choices.

<!-- better slide presentation -->
/** Notes **/

Now that you know how we workes let me addresses the core epistemological contribution of our research: ontological modeling is not merely a recording format; it is a historical practice in itself.

First, unlike traditional databases that require filled fields, our model relies on the **Open World Assumption**. In research on historical display, information is inherently fragmentary and contradictory. Our ontology allows us to document what we know, what we ignore, and to let multiple hypotheses coexist without forcing a premature conclusion.

Second, we had to resolve a major conceptual tension: how to articulate the **spatial dimension** (building topology, relations between works) with the **event-based dimension** (the exhibition as a dated curatorial act)? We use the mechanism of **multiple instantiation**. The same object is simultaneously a spatial entity (in BOT) and a historical artifact (in CIDOC-CRM). This allows fluid navigation between the geometry of the hanging and the history of the work.

Finally, and perhaps most powerfully for the historian, the descriptive logic of OWL enables **automatic reasoning**. By defining transitive or inverse properties, the system deduces relations that the historian did not explicitly encode. For instance, if Work A is left of B, and B left of C, the system infers the relation between A and C. This transforms the model into a testing ground: it can reveal contradictions in sources or make visible spatial configurations invisible to a linear reading of archives.

Thus, building the ontology forced us to theorize the exhibition not as a list of works, but as a system of significant topological relations.

===vvvvvv===

## Reasoning on Uncertainty

<!-- have an exemple of Feux pales-->

/** Notes **/

Let's look at how this applies concretely to the *Feux pâles* corpus. The documentation is fragmented: a photograph suggests one arrangement, a floor plan another, and a checklist yet another.

In a standard database, you would have to choose one "truth" or create messy comments fields. In Display, we model these as **alternative hypotheses**. The Open World Assumption allows these contradictions to exist side-by-side while we investigate.

The inference engine then goes to work. We don't just store data; we generate new knowledge. 
- If I state that a sculpture is `below` a painting, the system automatically knows the painting is `above` the sculpture. 
- If I define a gallery as `containing` a sub-space, and that sub-space `contains` a work, the system infers that the gallery `contains` the work, even if I didn't state it directly.

This is crucial for historical research. It allows us to query the data for implicit relationships and, importantly, to detect when two sources are logically incompatible. The model becomes an active partner in the interpretation process, not just a passive storage container.

===vvvvvv===

## From Data Entry to Historical Reasoning


<!-- have a schema -->

/** Notes **/

Traditionally, digital tools in museums have been used to create static archives of "known facts." If a fact was uncertain, it was often excluded or hidden. 

Our approach flips this. The ontology is a **dynamic environment**. It is a space where we can test hypotheses, visualize the logical consequences of our interpretations, and reason about the gaps in our knowledge. 

By formalizing the topological relations of the hanging, we are not just digitizing an exhibition plan; we are making the *curatorial logic* of the exhibition computable and analyzable. This turns the modeling process itself into a critical act of historical interpretation.

## Results and Assessment

- Validation on the *Feux pâles* corpus
- Making the semantic web practical for humanities research
- Structured, interoperable, and sustainable data

/** Notes **/

Applying Display allowed us to validate the central hypothesis across several types of documentary situations in *Feux pâles*: works with uncertain positions, partial photographic sources requiring interpretation, and contradictions between sources. In each case, Display enabled us to formulate and document multiple reconstruction hypotheses, explicitly stating the sources used and the degree of certainty associated with each.

3D visualization proved useful not as an end in itself but as a tool for comparing hypotheses: spatial implications that were not immediately apparent in two-dimensional archival documentation became visible once the configurations were modeled in three dimensions.

The methodological contribution may be the most important in the long term. Display demonstrates that it is possible to design an interface that hides ontological complexity without betraying it, and that researchers without semantic web expertise can produce high-quality data if the interface is designed based on their actual practices.

In terms of sustainability, the use of open standards—OWL, RDF, SPARQL—and open-source development ensures good information documentation and facilitates the preservation and reuse of data. Compatibility with CIDOC-CRM and Linked Art ensures interoperability with existing cultural heritage documentation systems. The data produced is not captive to an application: it exists independently of Display.

## Perspectives

- Enhancing the inference engine, automatic extraction from photographic archives (AI)
- Large-scale analysis, identification of curatorial patterns
- Annotation of triples (RDF*)
- Beyond hanging: toward a more comprehensive documentation of the exhibition experience

/** Notes **/

The goal was to create a proof of concept. Our development perspectives are organized across several horizons.

For example, enhancing the inference engine would allow for more sophisticated spatial reasoning. Integrating artificial intelligence mechanisms for the automatic extraction of spatial information from archive photographs is a promising avenue—reducing data entry time is a condition for the tool's adoption and still requires some adjustments.

The accumulation of structured data on multiple hangings opens the door to computational analyses that are not yet possible due to a lack of data: identification of curatorial patterns, study of the evolution of hanging practices, and comparisons between institutions over long periods.

The use of RDF-Star (RDF*) was explored to granularly record the sources of registered information. However, to simplify the development of the first version of the application, we decided to focus on the versions of the exhibition treated with named graphs and the inference engine.

Our next goal comes from my doctoral research I'l present tomorrow. Display models the hanging, the spatial dimension of the exhibition. But an exhibition consists of more than just a hanging. It also forms a discursive device, a temporal sequence, a set of curatorial decisions, and a visitor experience. The challenge for future developments is to extend the model to these other dimensions without losing the formal rigor that gives value to the work already accomplished.
